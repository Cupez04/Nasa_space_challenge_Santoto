This study describes the preparation and selection of mice for the Bion-M 1 biosatellite mission. Researchers trained mice to adapt to spaceflight conditions, including confinement, feeding systems, and launch/landing simulations. Through behavioral and health assessments, the most resilient mice were selected. Results showed reduced stress and stable health during the mission. The study’s impact lies in establishing a reliable protocol for animal welfare and scientific integrity in future space biology experiments.

This study explores how microgravity affects cellular biology and its implications for human health, using simulated microgravity models in mice. Researchers observed disruptions in gut microbiota composition, liver and brain metabolism, and tissue structure. Techniques included 3D clinostat simulation, 16S sequencing, and metabolomics. Results showed abnormal gut morphology, imbalanced serum metabolites, and altered liver and brain function. The findings highlight microgravity’s systemic impact and support the use of rodent models to study spaceflight-induced physiological changes.

This study validated a new system for isolating RNA and performing multiplex quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR) aboard the International Space Station. Researchers tested the system’s ability to extract high-quality RNA and analyze gene expression in microgravity. The method involved using lyophilized reagents and compact hardware suitable for spaceflight. Results confirmed successful RNA isolation and accurate gene expression profiling in orbit. The impact of this work is significant for enabling autonomous molecular diagnostics and biological research during space missions.

This study examined how spaceflight affects gene expression in mouse heart tissue, focusing on oxidative stress and cell cycle regulation. Researchers found that spaceflight upregulates genes linked to cellular stress and downregulates genes involved in cell cycle progression. These changes suggest that microgravity may impair heart cell renewal and increase oxidative damage. The findings help identify molecular pathways affected by spaceflight, which are important for protecting cardiovascular health during long-duration missions.

This study examined how different doses and types of space-like radiation affect oxidative stress in bone tissue. Using mouse models, researchers found that high-energy ions (like iron) and higher radiation doses increased oxidative damage and disrupted bone remodeling. The findings suggest that radiation exposure in space can impair skeletal health, and that both dose and ion type are critical factors in assessing astronaut risk.

This article outlines NASA’s efforts to translate basic life sciences research into practical medical solutions for human space exploration. It highlights how studies on microgravity, radiation, and isolation inform countermeasures for astronaut health. The research spans molecular biology, physiology, and behavioral science, aiming to support long-duration missions. The impact lies in bridging lab discoveries with real-world applications, ensuring crew safety and mission success in deep space environments.

This study developed a precise method for applying cyclic mechanical loads to small-animal vertebrae to evaluate bone quality. Using a custom loading device and imaging techniques, researchers measured vertebral response to repeated stress. The method allows for accurate assessment of bone strength, fatigue resistance, and microdamage. It’s valuable for studying bone diseases and testing treatments in preclinical models.

This study examined how ex vivo ionizing radiation affects collagen structure and mechanical properties of mouse vertebrae. Researchers found that radiation alters collagen organization and reduces bone strength, especially at higher doses. These changes impair bone quality and increase fracture risk. The findings are important for understanding radiation’s impact on skeletal health during space missions.
